Thousands of Crowly Users Lose Investments After Company Cites Obscure Terms of Service Clause

Investment platform users discover they unknowingly agreed to let the company "borrow" their funds indefinitely for "market research purposes"

Sarah Mitchell
March 15, 2025
5 min read

In a shocking turn of events that has left the investment community reeling, over 47,000 users of the popular micro-investment platform Crowly discovered this week that their collective $23.7 million in investments had been "temporarily relocated" by the company under a previously overlooked clause buried deep within the platform's 847-page terms of service agreement.

Users are now discovering that Section 7.3.2(b) of the terms, nestled between clauses about cookie preferences and push notification settings, grants Crowly "the irrevocable right to temporarily redistribute user assets for enhanced market research, competitive analysis, and executive team building exercises, including but not limited to company retreats in non-extradition territories."

"I thought I was investing in index funds, not funding someone's gap year in the Maldives," said Jennifer Walsh, a 34-year-old teacher who lost her entire $8,000 emergency fund. "Apparently, my money is now contributing to 'market research' at a five-star resort."

The clause, written in what legal experts are calling "weaponized legalese," was reportedly drafted by the same law firm that helped a major social media company claim ownership of users' firstborn children (a case that was later settled out of court for an undisclosed amount of cryptocurrency).

The Fine Print Strikes Again

Crowly CEO Marcus "Tank" Thompson defended the company's actions in a statement released from what appears to be a yacht in international waters. "Our users clearly consented to these terms when they clicked 'I Agree' without reading," Thompson explained via satellite phone. "We're simply exercising our legal right to optimize their investment strategy through hands-on market immersion techniques."

The company's head of legal affairs, Victoria Loophole (formerly a professional escape room designer), elaborated: "Section 7.3.2(b) is perfectly clear to anyone who reads the fine print with a magnifying glass while standing on one foot during a lunar eclipse. We even included helpful footnotes in 2-point font for clarity."

Key Points from Section 7.3.2(b):

  • • Company may "borrow" user funds for market research lasting 2-84 months
  • • Research includes "comprehensive analysis of luxury hospitality markets"
  • • Users granted "exclusive viewing rights" to research via company Instagram stories
  • • Refunds available only during the second Tuesday of months with 31 days that fall on a leap year

Community Response

The Crowly user community, previously known for their enthusiastic discussions about portfolio diversification, has now transformed into what can only be described as a support group for people who trusted an app with a cartoon crow as its mascot.

"I should have known something was up when the app's privacy policy included a section about 'treasure hunting' and 'finders keepers,'" lamented Robert Chen, who lost his wedding savings. "But the crow mascot was so friendly!"

Legal experts suggest that affected users may have limited recourse, as the terms of service were agreed to using the company's proprietary "QuickClick Consent Technology," which recorded each user's agreement timestamp, IP address, and what the company claims is "implied enthusiastic consent based on clicking speed."

As of press time, Crowly's customer service chatbot continues to respond to user complaints with auto-generated messages about "exciting investment opportunities in marine real estate" and links to a livestream of dolphins that the company claims are "portfolio managers in training."

This is a developing story. Updates will be provided as more information becomes available, assuming our own investment portfolios remain intact.
